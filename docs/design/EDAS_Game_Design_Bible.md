# EDAS
### A Game Design Bible — Second Edition
*Story-Driven 2D Adventure RPG · Puzzle Adventure · Educational Fantasy · Exploration*

---

## A Note Before We Begin

You asked for this to be greater than Undertale. I want to be honest about what that actually requires, because "greater than Undertale" isn't a tone you can sprinkle on top — it's a structural promise. Undertale is beloved for four specific, achievable things: a cast so well-written that players grieve NPCs they only met for ten minutes; a world that rewards being *looked at closely*, not just walked through; mechanics that are never just mechanics — they always mean something; and a game that remembers what the player did and treats that as sacred.

This edition is built to compete on exactly those four things, honestly, without copying a single mechanic, character, or story beat from Undertale or any other named inspiration. What's new in this pass:

- **The cast has nearly doubled**, and every new character exists to be loved, not just to deliver a mechanic.
- **The world got a second layer of mystery** — a "why did this all really happen" beneath the "why did this all happen," seeded from the very first region.
- **A hidden layer now tracks how the player treats the world** — not a morality meter, not a hidden genocide route, something quieter and more original: the game notices whether you *paid attention*, and it answers that back at the very end.
- **An optional true final challenge** exists now for players who want EDAS to test them the way Undertale's hardest secrets test its players — entirely optional, zero required content locked behind it.
- **Fen Solenne is deeper and sadder**, with his own supporting cast and his own tragedy that happened *before* CoCo, which recontextualizes everything he does to her.

Everything from the first edition that already worked — the opening, the six regions, the Title system tied to CoCo's visual stages — is preserved. This document builds on top of it rather than replacing it.

## 1. CORE VISION

EDAS is a game about what happens to the things we forget — and, in this edition, about the people who forget *with* us, on purpose, because it's easier than remembering.

The player is not sent on a quest to "learn subjects." The player is trapped, and the only way home is to help a broken world — and a broken girl, and the broken man who broke her, and a small cast of the world's other lost residents — remember. Every puzzle the player solves is something someone forgets and the player rediscovers *with* them, so the intellectual stakes are always, without exception, emotional stakes wearing a costume.

**The test for every system in this document:** if you removed the "educational" framing entirely, would the story, the cast, and the world still be worth finishing? If the answer is no, the system gets rebuilt until the answer is yes.

## 2. DESIGN PILLARS

### Pillar I — Wonder Before Instruction
The player should always encounter the *mystery* of a mechanic before the *explanation* of it.

### Pillar II — CoCo Is the Point, But She's Not Alone
Every system serves CoCo's arc — but this edition adds a second rule: **every named NPC deserves a fan.** If a character exists only to gate a mechanic, they get cut or rebuilt until someone on the team would genuinely be sad to see them go.

### Pillar III — Mastery, Not Memorization
Understanding is proven by *doing*, never by a quiz.

### Pillar IV — Curiosity Is Rewarded, Never Required
The critical path is complete without extra exploration. The game's *soul* is not.

### Pillar V — Small World, Deep World
Six interconnected regions, not an open world. Depth over size.

### Pillar VI — The Game Remembers You *(new)*
Nothing forces the player into a "route." But the game quietly tracks attentiveness — whether the player lingers, listens, revisits, finishes what they start — and the ending epilogue reflects it back, specifically and personally, without ever telling the player during play that they're being watched. See §17.

## 3. STORY

### 3.1 Opening — *(canonical, unchanged)*
The player competes in their school's Maze Championship. In a quiet corner of the hedge maze, they notice a patch of grass that doesn't match the rest. Beneath it: a stone hatch. Before they can investigate properly, another student rounds the corner and collides with them. They fall. The hatch seals above them. Silence.

They are trapped beneath the school, in a forgotten world built from abandoned knowledge. Soon they meet **CoCo**, a girl who remembers almost nothing about herself. Together they search for a way home, unaware that they are about to uncover the truth of a fractured timeline, a figure called **the Archivist**, and the real history buried under their own school.

### 3.2 The Shape of the Story — Now Two Descents, Not One
The player descends physically through six regions toward the Archive Spire, exactly as before. What's new: **the story now also descends through Fen's life**, one layer at a time, via a second thread of discovery running parallel to CoCo's memory restoration. Each region doesn't just restore a piece of CoCo — it also surfaces one piece of *Fen's* story, so that by the time the player meets him, he is not a mystery revealed in one late cutscene. He is a person the player has been quietly getting to know, region by region, the whole time, without realizing that's what the Cistern's old blueprints or the Hall of Ever-After's mismatched portraits were actually building toward.

### 3.3 The Second Mystery — "Who Else Fell?"
Seeded from the very first region and paid off across the whole game: the player is not the first person to find that patch of grass. Environmental storytelling — a second, smaller satchel half-buried in the Sunken Primer; a child-sized handprint in the Cistern that doesn't match CoCo's; a name carved and then deliberately scratched out in the Hall of Ever-After — builds a slow-burn question: **how many students has this school quietly lost, over how many decades, and why did none of them come back?**

The answer, saved for the Archive Spire, is not a horror twist. It's a heartbreak: every one of them *did* try to leave, and every one of them succeeded — except one. Sprocket (§6.5) is the last piece of that mystery, and finding him early without realizing what he is (see below) is one of the game's best-kept structural secrets.

### 3.4 The Central Relationship
Unchanged in spirit, deepened in execution: CoCo does not know she needs saving. The player's presence — someone who is *supposed* to forget, the way people on the surface do — is what destabilizes her missing memories. This edition adds one more beat: **CoCo starts, quietly, being afraid of what she'll be like once she remembers everything.** She's not just recovering herself. Partway through the game, she starts wondering whether she'll still like the person the player has been getting to know — a fear the player can never fully resolve for her, only stand beside her through, which is exactly what the best companion writing does.

### 3.5 The Antagonist Reframed, Again
Fen Solenne is not fought because he is evil, and — new this edition — he is not fought because he made one enormous mistake either. He is fought because he made a *smaller* mistake first, years earlier, that convinced him a much larger one would be safe. See Lore §4.6.

## 4. LORE & TIMELINE

### 4.1 What Edas Was
*(unchanged)* — "Edas" was the working name of a research collective founded by teacher-scholar **Fen Solenne**. EDAS stood for **"Everything, Documented And Saved."** His device, the **Perpetual Index**, could pull a fading memory from a mind and store it forever, unchanged.

### 4.2 The Student Who Was Saved Too Well
*(updated for §5.0's gender/naming system)* — Fen's brightest student, facing a rare degenerative memory condition, was indexed as an act of desperate love. The device had never held a living, ongoing mind before. It didn't know how to stop. What was left, wandering and nameless, became **CoCo** by default — a name self-given, echoed from a lullaby fragment that never fully indexed — or whatever name the player chooses at first meeting. Their true name, withheld from everyone including the player until the True Ending, is **Cosima** or **Cosimo**, depending on the presentation selected.

### 4.3 The Fracture
*(unchanged)* — Fen's attempt to reverse the Index fractured time itself inside Edas. Rooms began holding multiple decades at once. Lessons collided and corrupted into the puzzles, wildlife, and misconceptions the player finds throughout the world.

### 4.4 The Archivist Now
*(unchanged, restated for continuity)* — Fen did not become a villain who wants to destroy anything. He became a man who cannot let go, believing total preservation is total safety. His defeat, in the true path, is not destruction but *being understood* — the Index finished properly, the way it should have worked from the start.

### 4.5 Why the Player Fell
*(unchanged)* — The hatch has quietly resurfaced in the same spot for decades, because the school was built directly above the original Edas facility.

### 4.6 NEW — The First Attempt: Sprocket
Two years before CoCo, Fen built something else first: a small, articulate construct made from a broken pencil stub, a folded scrap of chalk, and a very great deal of borrowed brilliance, intended purely as a safety test for the Perpetual Index — could it hold a mind at all, without harming a living person? It worked. **Sprocket** woke up, thought, joked, remembered — and was, by every measure Fen had, *completely fine.*

What Fen didn't understand yet, and what the game only reveals near its end, is that Sprocket was fine specifically **because he had never been alive to begin with.** A constructed mind has nothing underneath the Index to protect — no messy, ongoing selfhood to conflict with a device built to hold things still. Fen mistook a clean experimental result for a safe one. He used the success of Sprocket to justify the far greater risk of using the Index on a real, living, changing girl.

**This is the tragedy underneath the tragedy:** Fen didn't gamble recklessly with CoCo's mind. He thought he'd already proven it was safe. His confidence was earned honestly, tested carefully, and still catastrophically wrong — which is a far more devastating shape of guilt than simple hubris, and gives the endgame confrontation somewhere to go that a simple "mad scientist" story never could.

Sprocket has known this the entire time. He has also, out of something between guilt and grief, spent every year since quietly looking after CoCo from a careful distance, unable to fix her, unable to leave her, unable to tell Fen what he now understands about what he actually is. When the player meets Sprocket early in the Sunken Primer as a wisecracking, seemingly minor helper NPC, none of this is visible yet. That's the point.

### 4.7 The Timeline, Fully Assembled
1. **~30 years before the story:** Fen Solenne founds the Edas research collective; begins work on the Perpetual Index.
2. **~22 years before:** The Index is completed in prototype form. Fen creates Sprocket to test it.
3. **~20 years before:** Fen's student — the girl who will become CoCo — begins losing her own memories to a degenerative condition.
4. **~19 years before:** Fen uses the Index on her. It does not stop indexing. Her selfhood comes apart; CoCo emerges, nameless.
5. **~19 years before, weeks later:** Fen attempts to reverse the Index. The timeline fractures. Edas becomes the world the player now explores.
6. **Across the following 19 years:** A slow trickle of students, across different decades, discover the same patch of grass above the same buried facility. Every one of them eventually finds a way out — except the player's predecessor by exactly one year, whose fate is the game's saddest optional discovery (see §6.8, **The Understudy**, recontextualized).
7. **Present day:** The player falls.

## 5. COCO
*(Full visual specification lives in the companion document `CoCo_Character_Design_Bible.md`. This section covers personality and story function only.)*

### 5.0 NEW — Player Choice: Presentation & Naming

**Gender selection:** at the moment the player first meets her/him — not before — the game offers a simple choice of presentation: the character the player has been traveling toward is shown in both forms, and the player picks which one continues the story. This is framed as a small, warm beat, not a menu screen buried in settings: the world itself has been shaping her/him out of fragments, and this is the moment it settles.

**Naming — locked flow:**
1. Before this scene, the character is referred to only as "the girl" / "the boy" in narration and UI — no name field exists yet, because narratively there's nothing to name yet.
2. At first meeting, she/he asks the player directly: *"Do you have a name for me? I don't have mine anymore."* A text field opens.
3. Leaving it blank defaults to **CoCo**, regardless of which presentation was chosen — CoCo is not gendered, which is part of why it works as a default either way.
4. Once set, the nickname is used for the rest of the story text. A **Rename option in Settings** exists for mobile-typo quality-of-life, since players will mistype under time pressure and shouldn't be stuck with it.

**Why the true-name reveal is protected regardless of nickname:** the True Ending's name reveal is written as two separate layers — the **nickname** (whatever the player typed, used throughout play) and the **true name** (fixed, story-owned, revealed only at the very end by Fen). This means no player input can ever accidentally undercut that ending beat. The true name is:
- **Cosima**, for the girl presentation
- **Cosimo**, for the boy presentation — same root, same lullaby-origin logic (see Lore §4.2), same emotional weight, chosen specifically so the reveal scene needs no rewriting depending on which presentation was picked.

**Internal writing rule:** all story-bible and script language about this character defaults to **"they"** internally in design docs (as used throughout this section going forward), resolving automatically to she/he in the actual game text based on the player's choice. This keeps future writing consistent without doubling every line of dialogue by hand.

### 5.1 Personality — Now With a Named Fear
*(previous stages of their arc are unchanged)* — Playfully evasive early, then unsettlingly perceptive, then grieving, then present. New this edition: starting in the mid-game (around the Hall of Ever-After), CoCo develops a specific, nameable fear rather than a general sadness — **they're afraid that the "real" them, once fully restored, won't want to be friends with the player the way this half-remembered version does.** This fear should surface in small, deniable ways (a joke that lands a beat too hard, a sudden reluctance to let the player see a new memory) rather than a single dramatic confession scene, and it's never fully resolved by dialogue — only by the player's continued presence, which is the whole point.

### 5.2 Visual Evolution
Governed entirely by the companion Character Design Bible's 8-stage system (Novice → Explorer → Seeker → Apprentice → Scholar → Sage → Archivist → Keeper of Memories). No changes this edition — that document is locked as canon.

## 6. THE ENSEMBLE CAST

This is the single biggest expansion in this edition. The original document had four named NPCs. That was too few to compete with a cast players fall in love with. EDAS now has **eight**, each load-bearing, each with a real want, a real joke, and a real reason to matter by the credits.

### 6.1 Bram, the Unbound Sentence *(Sunken Primer)*
A run-on sentence given a friendly, breathless voice, unable to stop talking in one long unpunctuated ramble until the player helps him find his missing period. **Function:** the game's first taste of environmental wordplay-as-character, and comic relief that never punches down — Bram is never mocked for how he talks, only helped.
**Small quest:** Find his period, scattered as a tiny, easy-to-miss dot-shaped collectible hidden in three possible rooms (randomized per playthrough, so returning players can't just walk straight to it — a small, cheap way to reward a second visit).
**Payoff:** Once punctuated, Bram speaks in short, oddly profound sentences for the rest of the game — a genuinely funny, genuinely sweet permanent character change that players will not expect from a joke NPC.

### 6.2 Nib *(Count's Hollow)*
A tiny, prickly fruit-creature convinced he's worth exactly half of everything around him, from an old, unresolved playground argument he can no longer fully remember the shape of.
**Quest:** Help him recover the memory (a Memory Echo chain), which turns out to be small and almost embarrassingly ordinary — a friend once got the bigger slice of something, once, and it never stopped stinging.
**Payoff:** Resolved with warmth, not correction — the player doesn't get to "fix" Nib's math, they get to sit with him while he realizes the argument was never really about the slice.

### 6.3 The Understudy *(Hall of Ever-After)* — Recontextualized
Originally written as a girl stuck reliving five minutes of an old class presentation. **New lore layer:** she is now explicitly tied to §4.7 — she is the last student before the player who found the hatch and never came back up, one year prior. She doesn't know this about herself; she believes, on a loop, that she's still waiting for her *actual* presentation to happen, still terrified of getting it wrong in front of an audience that no longer exists in any timeline she can reach.
**Resolution:** not solved by giving her confidence directly, but by the player simply staying and watching her get through it, once, all the way to the end, without interruption — the game's clearest lesson that presence is sometimes the entire intervention. A late, quiet line reveals (only to attentive players who piece it together with §3.3's environmental clues) that this is what actually happened to the missing satchel and scratched-out name found earlier in the game.

### 6.4 Old Wick *(Hearth Hollow)*
The hub's lantern-keeper — gruff, warm, endlessly patient with the hub's smaller lost creatures.
**Twist, unchanged from prior edition:** revealed for players who complete every region to be a memory of the player's own real-world school janitor — a lovely, quiet tie back to the surface without breaking the fourth wall.
**New this edition — a proper questline:** Old Wick is slowly forgetting how to light his own lanterns, and small side-favors across the game (fetching specific glowing motes from each region) slowly restore this ability, mirroring the player's own progression and giving the hub itself a visible, satisfying arc rather than remaining static set-dressing.

### 6.5 Sprocket *(first met — Sunken Primer; true nature — Archive Spire)*
A small, dry-witted construct made of chalk and pencil-stub, first encountered offering sarcastic, occasionally too-accurate commentary as the player solves the Sunken Primer's earliest puzzles. Functions early as pure comic relief and a light, deniable hint-giver — never explaining a puzzle outright, always nudging with a joke that happens to contain the answer if you're paying attention.
**The reveal (§4.6):** Sprocket is Fen's first, successful experiment — proof the Index could hold a mind safely, which is exactly what convinced Fen it was safe to try on a living girl. Sprocket has known this the whole game and has been quietly, guiltily looking after CoCo from a distance for years, unable to fix what happened, unable to leave.
**Why this works structurally:** the funniest character in the early game becomes, on replay, one of the saddest — every early joke has a second meaning once the player knows what he's carrying. This is the single strongest "reward for replaying" the cast offers, achieved with zero new content required, purely through recontextualization.
**Resolution:** Sprocket gets his own small optional arc in the Archive Spire — the chance, for the first time in nineteen years, to tell someone the truth. Whether the player encourages him to tell Fen directly affects one small, specific line of dialogue in Fen's final scene — a light-touch, low-cost way to make the player feel like the whole cast's fates are connected, not siloed by region.

### 6.6 Proctor Vale, the Prefect *(Hall of Ever-After)* — New
A former hall monitor, trapped decades before the Understudy, who has spent so long enforcing outdated rules on the world's smaller lost creatures ("No running in the Hall!" "Return that thought to where you found it!") that he's become genuinely feared by them — and privately terrified that if he ever stops enforcing rules, he'll have nothing left that proves who he used to be.
**Function:** comic antagonist for a small optional side-area (a rule-based stealth/logic puzzle sequence where the player must "follow the rules" to get past him, until they realize the smarter solution is to gently, respectfully break one on purpose).
**Payoff:** once out-thought, Vale doesn't become an ally by force — he becomes one because the player proved, kindly, that breaking a rule on purpose isn't the same as forgetting who you are. He relaxes, very slightly, for the rest of the game — small enough to be easy to miss, big enough to reward players who check back in on him.

### 6.7 Half-Page *(found in the Sunken Primer, follows the player after)* — New
A small, silent, loyal creature shaped like a folded, dog-eared page with two ink-blot eyes, discovered curled up in an abandoned desk. It attaches itself to the player after a very short, wordless rescue sequence and follows them for the rest of the game — never speaking, never gated behind a quest, purely present, purely comforting, reacting with small physical animations to major story beats (hiding during boss fights, pressing against the player's leg during CoCo's saddest scenes).
**The late reveal:** Half-Page is the physical form of a broken pinky-promise — a small, ordinary, decades-old vow between two students that was never kept. Finding out *whose* promise it was (tied into an optional Memory Echo chain) and giving the player a chance to finally complete the smallest possible version of it is one of the game's quietest, most devastating optional beats — deliberately modest in scale, because not every emotional payoff should be an operatic one.
**Design note:** Half-Page must never speak, never gain a "big" quest structure, and never become a combat pet. Its entire value is in *not* being a system — it is the game's clearest test of whether the team can let a character matter through presence alone.

### 6.8 Milo the Understudy's Rival *(Gallery of Unfinished Things)* — New
A half-finished sculpture of a boy, animated but incomplete — missing one arm, one eye, half a mouth — who was, in life, the Understudy's academic rival, and remains fiercely, comically competitive with a girl who (unbeknownst to him) has been stuck in the same five minutes for nineteen years.
**Function:** structural counterweight to the Understudy's quiet sadness — the Gallery region is meant to be the game's "breath" chapter (see original Chapter Outline), and Milo's rivalry-turned-friendship arc keeps that region genuinely funny even while it's laying groundwork for one of the saddest reveals in the back half.
**Payoff:** once the Understudy's true nature is revealed, Milo gets one exceptional scene — he doesn't get angry or grieve loudly, he simply finishes one small, specific unfinished piece of art *for* her, because competing with someone only means something if you also, quietly, believed they'd have finished first.

## 7. WORLD & REGIONS
*(Unchanged in structure and mechanics from the first edition — Hearth Hollow, Sunken Primer, Count's Hollow, Grammarwood, The Cistern, Hall of Ever-After, Gallery of Unfinished Things, Archive Spire. Full descriptions remain canon. This edition adds the following connective tissue.)*

### 7.1 NEW — The Second-Read Layer
Every region now contains **one deliberately small, easy-to-miss piece of Fen's personal history** (per §3.2), separate from its Memory Echo content: a signed blueprint corner in the Cistern, a child's height marked on a doorframe in the Sunken Primer with two sets of initials, a half-erased lesson plan in the Hall of Ever-After dated to "Sprocket's" creation. None of these are quest-gated. They exist purely for players doing a close read, and are catalogued together, only after the credits roll, in an unlockable "Director's Commentary" gallery mode (see §19.1) that rewards exactly the kind of attentive replay this bible is trying to earn.

### 7.2 NEW — A Second Face for Every Region
The core cast (§6) covers each region's headline character, but several regions were carrying that weight alone. This edition adds one modest, secondary presence to every region that didn't already have one — none of these are meant to rival the headline cast in screen time; they exist so every region feels inhabited on first arrival, not just once a quest starts.

**Grammarwood — Serif & Sans.** Two letterform siblings who bicker constantly and are never seen apart — Serif formal and anxious about looking "proper," Sans loose-limbed and allergic to standing still. Their running argument (readable as a light contrast/style puzzle — which of them can fit through which gap, which one a given sign "trusts") is comic relief for a region that's otherwise about the discomfort of not being able to read something correctly. Neither has a reveal or a sad arc; they're here to be a little annoying and a little beloved, on purpose.

**The Cistern — Plinth.** A small stone drain-guardian shaped like a squat frog, who has forgotten the rhyming password that lets water flow through the region and is deeply, quietly embarrassed about it. The player helps him remember it piece by piece via a simple call-and-response rhyme-matching sequence. Plinth never leaves his plinth (the name is not subtle, and he knows it, and would like it noted that he did not choose it).

**Count's Hollow — Carry.** A small, anxious digit who rides along during long division and lives in constant low-grade fear of being forgotten at the end of a problem. Functions as a nervous, well-meaning sidekick to Nib's quieter sadness (§6.2) — where Nib's arc is about an argument that was never really about the slice, Carry's is smaller still: the simple, repeated reassurance that being carried isn't the same as being a burden.

**Hall of Ever-After — The Substitute.** A loose stack of hall passes and late-arrival forms, animated into something like a person, who has been perpetually "filling in" for a teacher who left decades ago and has, over time, forgotten it isn't really their classroom. Gentler counterweight to Proctor Vale's rule-enforcement comedy (§6.6) — the Substitute doesn't enforce anything, just anxiously tries to keep a lesson going that no one is left to attend, and is quietly relieved rather than devastated when the player tells them, kindly, that it's all right to stop.

**Gallery of Unfinished Things — The Critic.** An unfinished painting of a single eye, mounted crooked, that narrates the Gallery's exhibits in a voice that starts out cutting and gets steadily warmer the longer the player spends actually looking at things rather than rushing past them — a small, mechanical reward for exactly the kind of unhurried attention Pillar IV asks for. Never becomes a full character with a name or a quest; the joke and the warmth are both in the voice alone.

**Archive Spire — Marginalia.** Tiny handwritten notes living in the margins of every book in the Spire, grown faintly sentient from centuries of being read. They speak only in fragments — corrections, asides, the occasional "this part is true" — and become the player's most literal source of hints in the Spire's puzzles. Their existence is the clearest environmental foreshadowing of what happened to CoCo: the Index doesn't just store minds, it's been quietly generating small ones by accident for years.

## 8. BOSSES — MISCONCEPTIONS GIVEN FORM
*(The six regional bosses — The Marginal Man, Half-Light, The Confluence, The Loop, The Critic, and the Archivist himself — remain unchanged and canonical. This edition adds one new optional encounter.)*

### 8.1–8.5 *(unchanged, see original spec)*

### 8.6 The Archivist — Final Boss *(unchanged three-phase structure, restated)*
Phase One (The Collector) tests every mechanic learned. Phase Two (The Unraveling) requires **Time Weave** to stabilize a fracturing arena. Phase Three (The Choice) is not combat — Fen asks the player for one true, personal memory, and the response determines the ending (§16).

### 8.7 NEW — Optional Superboss: The Unspooled
Unlocked only after **100% Memory Echo completion** and the **Secret Ending** requirements (§16.3) are separately met — never required, never hinted at during normal play, discoverable only by players who go looking for one more thing after they think they're done.

**Concept:** every misconception the player has already disproven — the Marginal Man's literalism, Half-Light's greed, The Confluence's false causation, The Loop's repetition, The Critic's perfectionism — didn't disappear when defeated. It went somewhere. **The Unspooled** is all of it, recombined into a single, formless, constantly-shifting final encounter that borrows one mechanic from every prior boss, cycling unpredictably, with no telegraphed pattern repeating twice in the same fight.

**Design intent:** this is EDAS's answer to the kind of optional, brutally fair, mastery-testing final challenge that the best games save for players who finish everything else first. It rewards total fluency across every mechanic the game ever taught, and nothing else — no new lore is gated behind it (lore should never be a carrot for difficulty alone), only a cosmetic title (**"True Understanding"**) and a small, wordless final CG of every defeated boss's calmest resting state, together, at peace. The absence of any story reward is deliberate: this fight exists purely as a love letter to players who wanted the game to ask more of them, and pretending it's more than that would cheapen it.

## 15. MUSIC DIRECTION

Unchanged core concept — every track a variation on **CoCo's Theme**, a four-note phrase that never fully resolves until the True Ending. This edition adds:

**Sprocket's Theme:** a small, plinking, slightly-too-cheerful music-box variation of CoCo's motif, played one octave up and slightly out of time — funny on first listen, unmistakably grief-shaped on a second listen once the player knows what he is. This is the single clearest place in the whole score where the "replay recontextualizes everything" design goal should be audible, not just readable.

**The Understudy's Theme:** the same five bars looped, each loop adding one more instrument and one more layer of dissonance, never resolving until her arc resolves — a piece of music that is *structurally* stuck, not just thematically sad.

**Half-Page's Motif:** no melody at all — a single, warm, sustained low tone that appears underneath other tracks whenever it's on screen, felt more than heard, present the way it is.

## 16. ENDINGS

### 16.1 Normal Ending — *"Enough"* *(unchanged)*
Complete the critical path; decline to offer Fen a personal memory. CoCo stabilizes but doesn't fully complete her transformation. Gentle, bittersweet.

### 16.2 True Ending — *"Everything, Documented and Saved"* *(unchanged core requirement, epilogue expanded)*
Complete all six regions' lore fragments; offer Fen a real personal memory in Phase Three. CoCo is fully restored, named — **Cosima** — by Fen as an apology. **New this edition:** the epilogue now includes one short scene per rescued cast member (Bram, Nib, the Understudy, Old Wick, Sprocket, Vale, Half-Page, Milo), each shown finding their own small piece of peace — turning the True Ending from "the villain is redeemed" into "the whole world got to heal," which is the more ambitious, harder-earned ending this cast now deserves.

### 16.3 Secret Ending — *"The Lantern Keeper"* *(unchanged requirement)*
Find Old Wick's full backstory and choose to stay behind with CoCo. Unlocks eligibility for the Unspooled superboss (§8.7).

### 16.4 Completionist Ending — *"Every Corner"* *(unchanged requirement, expanded reward)*
100% Memory Echoes, all Expert Puzzles, all NPC quests, True Ending achieved. Reward now explicitly includes the full **Director's Commentary gallery** (§19.1) alongside the original epilogue illustration set.

### 16.5 NEW — The Quiet Coda
Not a fifth ending in the traditional sense — a short, unlockable **post-credits scene**, available regardless of which ending the player earned, that only plays if the game's attentiveness tracking (§17) registers a sufficiently high "presence" score. In it, the player — back on the surface, back at school — notices something small and strange in an entirely unrelated corner of their own real classroom. Nothing is explained. It's a two-line hook, not a sequel pitch, and it exists purely to reward the specific kind of player this whole bible has been trying to earn: the one who looked closely at everything.

## 17. NEW — "THE WAY YOU REMEMBER" (Hidden Attentiveness System)

This is the most structurally original addition in this edition, and it's built specifically to avoid copying any existing game's morality-route mechanics.

**What it is not:** a good/bad meter, a hidden kill-count, a pacifist/genocide fork, or anything the player can see, be warned about, or optimize for during play.

**What it is:** a single, quiet, invisible counter tracking a small set of attentiveness signals — whether the player finishes conversations rather than skipping them, whether they revisit NPCs after story beats rather than only meeting them once, whether they complete small optional kindnesses (Bram's period, Nib's quest, sitting through the Understudy's loop) rather than bypassing them once available, and whether they ever return to Hearth Hollow "just to visit" between story beats rather than only passing through.

**What it affects:** nothing during play. No dialogue changes, no difficulty changes, no locked content. Its *only* effect is on the specific wording and warmth of the ending epilogue scenes in §16.2 and the existence of §16.5's Quiet Coda. A player who rushed through the game still gets a complete, satisfying ending — just a slightly quieter one. A player who paid close attention gets an ending that feels, without ever saying so, like it knows them.

**Why this beats a traditional morality system:** it never asks the player to perform kindness for a reward, because they can't see it coming. It measures something closer to genuine engagement than to in-fiction virtue, which fits a game about a girl whose whole tragedy is that someone tried to *engineer* care instead of simply giving it.

## 18. PLAYER EMOTIONAL JOURNEY
*(Unchanged shape, restated)* — ordinary joy to disorientation at the fall; curiosity and warmth in the early game; growing unease and protectiveness in the mid game; real grief and real hope in close proximity late; a finale weighted by choice rather than difficulty; credits that always close on gold light, never darkness.

## 19. CHAPTER-BY-CHAPTER OUTLINE

1. **The Maze** — real-world opening; the fall.
2. **Waking Underground** — meeting CoCo and, in short order, Sprocket and Bram; Sunken Primer begins; earning *Explorer*.
3. **The Grass Remembers** — first hint the underground connects to the real school; Half-Page rescued; Count's Hollow opens; earning *Seeker*.
4. **What the Wind Won't Say** — Grammarwood; first real fracture sighting; earning *Apprentice*.
5. **Pressure** — The Cistern; the Confluence boss; first confirmation of "F. Solenne"; earning *Scholar*.
6. **Portraits** — Hall of Ever-After; the Understudy and Proctor Vale; CoCo's first full flashback; earning *Sage*.
7. **Unfinished** — Gallery of Unfinished Things; Milo; the game's tonal "breath" chapter; earning *Archivist*.
8. **What Sprocket Carried** — a short, quiet interstitial chapter, entirely optional in pacing weight but placed as mandatory story beat: Sprocket's true nature is revealed to the player (not yet to Fen), recontextualizing the entire cast's earlier scenes.
9. **Everything, Documented** — Archive Spire; the Archivist revealed in full; Phases One and Two.
10. **The Choice** — Phase Three; the ending branch; epilogue scenes for the full ensemble cast; credits.

### 19.1 NEW — Director's Commentary (Post-Game Unlockable Mode)
A gallery, unlocked at 100% completion, presenting every "second-read" clue from §7.1 alongside a short, warm, in-universe annotation — framed as CoCo, fully restored, narrating what each clue actually meant, now that she remembers. Built specifically to reward the kind of replay-and-reread engagement Undertale's best-loved secrets are famous for, without copying any of their specific content or delivery.

*(Unchanged phase structure from the first edition; scope updated to reflect the ensemble cast expansion.)*

- **Phase 0 — Vertical Slice (2–3 months):** Sunken Primer end-to-end, including Bram, Sprocket, Half-Page's rescue sequence, one Trial, one boss, CoCo's first two visual stages.

## 21. RISKS

- **Risk: an 8-character ensemble dilutes focus from CoCo.** *Mitigation:* strict rule — no ensemble character's screen time in any single region may exceed CoCo's; all ensemble arcs remain optional and regionally contained, never forking the critical path.
- **Risk: Sprocket's reveal feels like a cheap twist rather than an earned one.** *Mitigation:* the reveal must be supported by re-playable evidence (§19.1) proving it was fair all along — if a first-time player can't go back and find the clues themselves, the twist gets reworked before ship.
- **Risk: the hidden attentiveness system (§17) is discovered by players and treated as a min-maxable checklist, defeating its purpose.** *Mitigation:* never surface any UI, achievement, or percentage tied to it; if datamined, the marketing response is to acknowledge it exists in general terms without publishing its exact criteria, preserving its function for future players.
- **Risk: "greater than Undertale" as an internal north star causes feature creep chasing comparison rather than serving EDAS's own story.** *Mitigation:* every new system in this edition was justified by asking "does this serve CoCo, Fen, or the cast" before "does this compare well" — any future addition should pass the same test.
- *(All risks from the first edition — educational framing in marketing, puzzle difficulty calibration, CoCo pacing, mobile performance, the True Ending's memory-sharing mechanic — remain valid and unchanged.)*

## 22. SUGGESTIONS & IMPROVEMENTS IN THIS EDITION

- **Gave Fen a tragedy before his tragedy** (Sprocket, §4.6) — turning his guilt from "I made a terrible gamble" into "I did everything right and it still broke her," which is a harder, truer, more devastating shape of grief.
- **Quadrupled the ensemble cast** with each new character built around presence and want rather than mechanical gating — Half-Page in particular is a deliberate bet that a character who never speaks can still be one of the most beloved in the game.
- **Built a genuinely novel hidden-attentiveness system (§17)** rather than borrowing a morality-meter structure from any existing game, designed specifically to reward engagement without ever being visible or optimizable during play.
- **Added an entirely optional, zero-lore-reward superboss (§8.7)** for players who want EDAS to test mastery the way the genre's best optional content does, without turning story content into a difficulty-gated carrot.
- **Recontextualized an existing character (the Understudy) with new lore** rather than only adding new characters — proving the world was already dense enough to reward a second look, which is the whole thesis of this edition.
- **Recommend, if resources allow going forward,** a short, fully optional "Sprocket's Workshop" side-room in Hearth Hollow, unlocked post-game, where the construct putters around fixing small broken objects from the whole cast's stories — a low-cost, high-warmth coda space in the spirit of the games this bible is trying to stand alongside, built from nothing but characters players will already love by the time they'd see it.
